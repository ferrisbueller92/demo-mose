Five calls to make before formulas lock

These are the decisions the research surfaced that change the recipe — worth resolving with William (and, where noted, a food-safety/regulatory consult) before a co-packer quotes anything.

Highest-severity flag

PRIME's sodium is 4.5–10× a normal sports drink — per millilitre

Typical sports drinks carry ~40–85 mg sodium per 100 ml. PRIME's ~400 mg in a 60–100 ml vial lands at roughly 400–650 mg per 100 ml. At that concentration the taste stops being "salty" and tips into harsh/metallic (it starts triggering irritation pathways, not just intensity), and flavour masking alone won't fully hide it.

Recommendation: pick one — lower the sodium target toward a normal electrolyte dose, switch to sodium citrate (milder, doubles as a natural acidulant), or deliberately own a savoury/electrolyte-forward taste for PRIME rather than selling it as a fruity shot.

Compliance-critical

SETTLE's lavender must be aroma-level, not "Silexan-grade"

Silexan is a registered pharmaceutical lavender extract (80 mg/day for anxiety, a medicinal product in Germany). EU borderline-product law classifies by "minimal therapeutic dose" — use Silexan-grade material at anything near its therapeutic level in a drink and you invite a medicinal-product classification fight, the same trap melatonin already set for SETTLE.

Recommendation: resolve the brief's "Silexan-grade OR aroma" phrasing in favour of aroma-only — a food-grade lavender flavour at ppm levels. Confirm the exact level with a toxicology/regulatory consult, not just a taste panel.

Sweetener system

Monk fruit is out for the EU launch

Confirmed still not broadly authorised in the EU as of 2026 — one narrow aqueous-extract approval (Oct 2024), with a wider food-additive application still mid-EFSA-assessment (public consultation opened April 2026). A pending assessment is not a legal authorisation.

Recommendation: design the sweetness around steviol glycosides + thaumatin (thaumatin actively masks the bitter/metallic notes of caffeine, EAAs and magnesium — it isn't just a sweetener), with erythritol optional but note the >10% laxative-warning label rule.

Confirmed safe

The dry-cap mix-moment chemistry holds up

Creatine degrades to creatinine only over days at citrus pH (21% over 3 days at pH 3.5) — over a 30–60 minute drink window that's under 0.01%. The freshness story is real: the risk is a vial sitting mixed for days, which the format prevents by design.

One caveat to design for: 3 g creatine won't fully dissolve in ~100 ml (it needs ~225 ml). Micronised creatine + a co-processed carrier gives a cloudy-but-not-gritty drink — spec the mouthfeel/visual around a slightly milky pour, engineer against sandy sediment.

Format lock

Still base only — never sparkling

Dropping dry powder into a sealed carbonated vial and then shaking it is close to a worst-case geyser setup: the powder multiplies nucleation sites and the CO₂ erupts the instant the cap opens. This isn't a maybe — it's the mechanism behind the Mentos-and-cola effect.

Recommendation: every SKU is a still base. If a sparkling line is ever wanted, it needs a completely different (pre-mixed, no-shake) mechanic.

Liquid bases, ranked by how hard they are to ship shelf-stable

Because the actives stay dry in the cap, only the liquid needs to survive on a shelf — but that's still where the manufacturing risk lives.

Flavoured water easiest

Acidify to pH ~3.0–3.8, hot-fill or tunnel-pasteurise, dual preservative (potassium sorbate + sodium benzoate, each at lower ppm together than alone). Cheapest, most co-packer-proven route.

Citrus-tropical water easy

Naturally low pH does the preservation and the masking work at once — this is why the spec'd DRIVE/PRIME directions are the safe ones. Formulate from natural flavour compounds, not real juice, for batch consistency.

Berry base good

Naturally acidic (~3.0–3.4), preservation-friendly and a strong physical masker for SETTLE's magnesium. The open question is the tannin × magnesium-glycinate interaction — bench-test it.

Tea (matcha/jasmine) care

Near-neutral, so it needs deliberate acidifying, and matcha brings its own bitterness and caffeine to account for. Workable for an Asian line, but not a free flavour swap.

Diluted coconut water hard

Low-acid (pH >4.6) — the botulism-risk zone for ambient shelf products. Needs acidify-to-4.5 + validated thermal/preservative protocol signed off by a food-safety lab. Lime (as in the spec'd PRIME) helps.

Sparkling ruled out

Incompatible with shake-to-mix (see the geyser flag). Still bases only.

Mix-moment chemistry, confirmed

What actually happens in the ten seconds between pop and drink.

Creatine dissolution

3 g won't fully dissolve in ~100 ml (solubility ~13.3 g/L → needs ~225 ml). Micronised creatine + co-processed carrier dissolves within ~10 s of shaking with no visible grit — the realistic result is a slightly cloudy, milky pour, not a clear one. Spec the visual around that.

Creatine pH stability

Degradation to creatinine is 21% over 3 days at pH 3.5 — which back-calculates to <0.01% over a 30–60 min drink window. Citrus-acidic bases are fine on the timescale that matters; only a vial left mixed for days is a problem, which the format prevents.

EAA bitterness (DRIVE)

Leucine/isoleucine/valine are inherently bitter, leucine most of all. The proven masker is citrus acid + glycine — which is exactly why DRIVE's citrus-tropical direction is load-bearing, not stylistic. Thaumatin stacks on top.

Magnesium glycinate (SETTLE)

Tastes metallic/alkaline. Countered by acidic mixers (the berry base), cold serving, and glycine — which is already in SETTLE and does double duty: it's ~70% as sweet as sugar and actively masks bitterness and metallic notes.

Caffeine (PRIME/DRIVE)

50–75 mg in ~80 ml is several multiples over the bitterness threshold, so it needs active masking. Note: sugar suppresses caffeine bitterness better than non-caloric sweeteners — a real tension with the sugar-free positioning, which is where thaumatin earns its place.

Electrolyte placement

Put Na/K/Mg in the dry cap, not the liquid — matches the "actives stay dry" philosophy, keeps the liquid's pH/preservative chemistry simple, and use citrate forms throughout (milder, less bitter than chloride, and a natural acidulant).
